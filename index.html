Intro

I am a student at the University of Southern California (USC) in Los Angeles pursuing a Doctorate degree in Physical Chemistry. I joined USC in the fall of 2014 and have been working in the group of Prof. Andrey Vilesov since. Prof. Vilesov's group focuses on studying molecules in low temperature quantum liquids. USC also offers a wide range of cool topics for young researchers like ultrafast physics, gas phase dynamics and much more. Check out this page for more details.

Science can be fun if we understand and appreciate the real world objects through small matters that form it. For instance, a beautiful house is made out of bricks or wood which are its essence. On a similar analogy, tiny atoms and molecules make up the bulk of matter that exists on earth. Molecules are defined as atoms bonded together as large entities. The way how objects behave in various conditions is dictated by the molecular properties such as structure, size e.t.c. A good example is the bending of metal train tracks in summer as a result of expansion. So, people like me do "Spectroscopy" to study the structure and bonding of molecules using a light source (such as LASER). Such study can be difficult at room temperature conditions as the molecules tend to be unstable or highly movable. So, bringing their temperature down by trapping them in some sort of ultra cold vessel, one can immobilize these molecules. Once they have a restricted movement, a laser can be fired towards them and a change in their physical properties such as bond length, electronic structure, can be monitored by counting the number of photons (bricks that make up light) absorbed/released. I in particular, use helium nanodroplets as a cold vessel which can reach down to as low as 0.4 Kelvin, for such studies. For more details, check out my research page.

What I do

Helium (He) is a chemical with unique properties, such as superfluid and inert, which make it ideal for molecular spectroscopy. Cooled helium atoms, which are typically produced by adiabatically expanding helium gas through a small orifice, tend to coagulate and take shapes like droplets of nanometer sizes, and thus the name "Helium Nanodroplet". This gas phase matrix isolation technique offers subzero kelvin temperatures inside the droplets, where atoms/molecules are often trapped. As a result, a highly resolved molecular spectra can be acheived, along with the possibility of formation of exotic local minima molecular structures.

Helium Droplet Appratus

The helium droplet setup comprises of a series four vacuum chambers. The chambers are kept at low pressures (10-8 to 10-10 mbar) to avoid impurities, through turbomolecular pumps. Helium droplets are produced in the source chamber (Left) when helium gas is allowed to flow from a nozzle kept at ~3-10 K. Droplets can be generated in either pulsed ot continuous modes. Besides higher flux, pulsed valves can be advantageous for experiments with pulsed laser setups. After formation, droplets pass through a 2 mm skimmer (pink) into the pickup chambers. The pickup chambers act as a source of guest molecules which are introduced through a leak valve. As the helium droplets traverse the pickup chambers, they engulp the free guest molecules. The number of guest pickup is dictated by the partial pressure in the pickup chamber, which normally follows a Poisson statistics. Along with a leak valve, a beam shutter is also attached in the chamber for blokcing and unblocking of helium beam. The doped droplets then enter the detection chamber through a gate valve. This chamber holds a Quadrupole Mass Spectrometer (QMS) with electron inonizer which measures the mass distribution of the incomming beam. The size of a droplet is typically measured by monitoring the m/z = 8 signal on the QMS. During spectroscopy, when a laser at molecular resonant frequency strikes the droplets from the opposite direction, the droplet reduces in size beacuse of energy dissipation from the dopant to helium bath. The results in a decreased m/z = 8 signal at the resonant frequency. By this method, also known as "depletion spectroscopy", spectra of molecules can be obtained by measuring m/z = 8 signal as a function of laser frequency.

Pulsed nozzle source

There are two different modes of nozzle operation: continuous and pulsed. Continuous nozzles are more commonly used by spectroscopists while pulsed sources lack a detailed study. Research groups across the globe that use the two modes has been noted in a recent book chapter.

Pulsed nozzles are ideal for experiments such as laser induced fluorescence or multiphoton ionization with pulsed lasers and time-of-flight spectrometry. Compared to continuous nozzle sources, pulsed sources have a factor of ~ 100 larger flux output and the droplet sizes are an order of magnitude larger at similar source pressure (P0 ) and temperature (T0 ) conditions. However, due to electromechanical motion of the pulsed valve (to and fro motion of the poppet against a 1 mm orifice of the valve, as shown in the picture), the minimum (T0 ) that is achievable is higher as compared to a continuous source (9 K vs 3 K , respectively). This may pose a problem for a person who wants to study large moleules or clusters, as droplet size depends on the nozzle P0 and T0. Recent modifications to the pulsed nozzle attachment has enabled lower temperature operation down to ~ 4 K, with droplet sizes reaching ~ 1011 atoms. In the picture above, a copper enclosement with a pulsed valve fitting allows for a better heat exchange between the two, thereby reducing the ultimate nozzle temperature.

Selective formation of He4+

Electron impact ionization of superfluid helium droplets containing up-to several times 104 atoms produces a broad distribution of Hen+ ions that peaks at n = 2 and decreases monotonically towards larger n. In large droplets (say 105 or more atoms), however, the He4+ signal intensity is anomalously large. Our group has studied the mechanism of the formation of He4+ ions in large helium droplets by varying duration of the electron impact excitation pulse using a QMS and TOFMS. Measurements with difference electron bombardment pulse durations enabled us to discern a characteristic time for enhanced He4+ production in large droplets. A qualitative model is presented in a recently published article in which two He metastable atoms react on a droplet surface. The He+ ion cores each pick up a nearby He atom, resulting in a structure in which the two He2+ cores share a single Rydberg electron while minimizing repulsion between the cores.
